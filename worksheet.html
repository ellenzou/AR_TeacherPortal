    Worksheet #3: Worlds in our Solar System

    Four versions of this worksheet are provided.  It is recommended that you use all four sheets, but give only one sheet to each student.  In this way, a quarter of the class receives each sheet.  Different students become "experts" on different worlds, and are able to share their knowledge with their classmates.  This also prevents students from becoming bogged down in filling out several pages.

    The purpose of this worksheet is to get students thinking about the key characteristics that scientists use to describe a world.  This worksheet also focuses students on creating lists rather than writing lengthy descriptions.  You should suggest that students take similar notes in their notebooks on each world they study.

    When you go over the worksheets with the class, it is recommended that you only go over one world for each of the sheets  so that each student has an opportunity to contribute without the discussion becoming too long.  Attempt to engage students in discussing science concepts as you use this sheet rather than just focusing on the right answers.  Good topics for discussion include temperature scales and how to convert between them, gravity, what a magnetic field does for a world, and the reasons for seismic activity.

    Detailed information about each world is provided within the Solar System Database Section of The World of Alien Rescue of this digital manual.

    Worksheet #4: The Problem-Solving Process

    Assignment: Suppose you had to teach someone how to find a home for one species of aliens.  What would you tell him or her to do? Make a list of the steps you are using to solve the problem in Alien Rescue.

    Example Response:

    Step 1: Find out what one of the alien species needs.

    Step 2: Search the solar system database for worlds where that species might be able to live.

    Step 3: Come up with a hypothesis about where that species can live.

    Step 4: Figure out if we are missing any information we need to know in order to decide if the world can meet all the needs of that species.

    Step 5: Launch a probe to gather the information we need.

    Step 6: Check the data from the probe in the Control Room.

    Step 7: Decide if that world is a good home for that species.  If not, go back to step 2.

    Worksheet #5: Probe Design

    Four versions of this sheet are provided. You may wish to use two for guided practice and two for a quiz.

    The purpose of this worksheet is to help students to recognize that every probe should have a mission - a need for specific information, and that they must deal with constraints in order to design a probe that does not malfunction. When you go over these worksheets with the class, discuss how scientists design instruments to gather data that they want to collect. This is also a good time to get students to describe what type of data each instrument can collect.

    Task: You want to find out if any of the moons of Jupiter have magnetic fields. You have a tight budget, so you need to design the least expensive probe possible. Design a probe by putting check marks next to those items you would include.


    Example Solution

    1. Probe type: Flyby

    2. Power source: Solar panels

    3. Communication: High gain antenna

    4. Instruments: Magentometer


    Task: You are considering Titan as a possible home for one of the alien species, and you need to know its atmospheric pressure, what its atmosphere is made of, and whether or not it has earthquakes. Design a probe by putting check marks next to those items you would include.


    Example Solution

    1. Probe type: Lander

    2. Power source: Thermoelectric generator

    3. Communication: High gain antenna

    4. Instruments: Barometer, spectrograph, seismograph


    Task: You would like to design the least expensive probe possible to find out more about temperature ranges of several areas on Mercury. Put check marks next to those items you would include.


    Example Solution

    1. Probe type: Orbiter

    2. Power source: Solar panel or solar panel with battery

    3. Communication: Low gain antenna

    4. Instruments: Infrared camera


    Task: You think that Pluto may be a good home for the Akona, but need to know if it has water or earthquakes. You would also like to take pictures of broad areas of its surface to look for possible mountain ranges. Design a probe by putting check marks next to those items you would include.


    Example Solution

    1. Probe type: Lander

    2. Power source: Thermoelectric generator

    3. Communication: High gain antenna

    4. Instruments: Spectrograph, seismograph, wide-angle camera

    Worksheet #6: How Scientists Work

    Assignment:

    How do scientists work? Think about how you have been working during Alien Rescue and consider how it is similar to the way you think scientists work. On the back of this sheet, list at least four different facts you think describe how scientists work. For each fact, explain what you mean and why you think itâ€™s important.

    Example Solution:

    1.   Scientists collaborate. By working together, they can get more done and combine skills.
    2.   Scientists make hypotheses. These hypotheses guide their investigation.
    3.   Scientists rarely get it right the first time. They make many mistakes and learn from each one.
    4.  Scientists always back up their opinions with facts and data. They never simply say they believe something without being able to explain why, and this helps them be trusted.
    5.   Scientists publish their findings. In this way they add to what other scientists know. Others build on this information.

    Worksheet #7: Interpreting Data

    The purpose of this worksheet is to get students to take raw data and turn it into information they can apply to developing a solution. Discussion of this worksheet can again focus on characteristics scientists consider when they study a world, including atmospheric density and composition, magnetic field, and seismic activity. Or, you can focus on reviewing the types of data each instrument included in the probe design room collects.

    Students' answers should have two parts - an interpretation of the data and some application of it to the problem. The following are sample responses, though students may come up with a rich variety of applications.

    Picture 1: Barometer reading taken on Titan This is a completed example to help students understand the desired format of their response.

    Picture 2: Barometer reading taken on Callisto Callisto has a very thin atmosphere (1/100 of Earth's atmosphere). This would make it a poor choice for a species like the Wroft who need a thick atmosphere, but it might be alright for other species who need less of an atmosphere, like the Akona and the Sylcari.

    Picture 3: Magnetometer reading for Jupiter Jupiter has a very strong magnetic field. This would make it a poor choice for the Wroft because magnetic fields are dangerous for them.

    Picture 4: Magnetometer reading for Mars Mars has a weak magnetic field, but it would be enough to protect the surface from the solar wind. This means the Eolani could live there.

    Picture 5: Seismograph reading from Deimos Deimos has very little seismic activity. The Eolani, Kaylid, and Akona all need to find worlds with low seismic activity, so this might be a good home for them.

    Picture 6: Seismograph reading from Io Io has strong earthquakes. Only a species who can survive earthquakes, like the Wroft, Jakala-Tay, or Sylcari, could live there.

    Picture 7: Spectrograph reading on Neptune Neptune has hydrogen, helium, and methane. Since methane has carbon in it, this might be a good home for the Wroft.

    Worksheet #8: Science Topics

    Assignment: Pick four of the topics listed here and write what you know about them. Make sure you write at least two or three sentences about each. Continue on the back of this sheet.

    Example Solution:

    Temperature Scales: There are three temperature scales: Fahrenheit, Celsius, and Kelvin. In the Celsius scale, water freezes at 0 degrees and boils at 100 degrees. In the Kelvin scale, zero stands for absolute zero, the point where molecules stop moving. To change Celsius to Kelvin, add 273.

    Craters: Craters are formed when a meteorite hits the ground. Atmospheres, liquid water, and volcanic eruptions can fill in craters. If a world has a lot of craters, it means that its surface has not changed in millions of years. We can conclude that those worlds donâ€™t have atmospheres, liquid water, or active volcanoes.

    Gravity: Gravity is a force. The more mass an object has, the greater its gravity. If we go to a world with less gravity than we are used to we can jump higher and move more easily than on Earth. We would also weigh less. If we went to a world with higher gravity, we would find it difficult to move.

    Seismic Activity: If a world has a liquid core and a hard surface, then pressure can build up, and this can make the surface shake. The result is earthquakes. Volcanoes are also the result of seismic activity.

    Atmospheres: An atmosphere is a blanket of gases that surrounds a world. Atmospheres can be made up of many different types of gases, and the atmospheres of other worlds are different from Earthâ€™s. A barometer can be used to measure the atmospheric pressure. Small worlds, like Phobos and Deimos, donâ€™t have enough gravity to hold onto an atmosphere.

    Magnetic Fields: If a world has a liquid metalic core, then it creates a magnetic field around that world. A magnetic field can protect a world from the solar wind. The solar wind is made up of particles blown off of the sun. If Earth did not have a magnetic field, life would not be possible because of the solar wind.
