    Barometer

    Barometers measure the density of an atmosphere, not how far an atmosphere extends from the surface of a world. Earth's atmosphere is defined as 1.0, so a reading that is less than 1.0 signifies an atmosphere that is less dense than Earth's.




    Callisto has a very thin atmosphere, about one-hundredth of Earth's.
    Titan has an atmosphere that is somewhat thicker (denser) than Earth's. It therefore has a higher atmospheric pressure.
    Venus has an extremely dense atmosphere. It would crush you if you stood on the surface of Venus.

    Magnetometer

    Magnetometers measure the strength of a world's magnetic field. Only worlds with liquid cores made of a metal such as iron have magnetic fields. A magnetic field protects a world from the solar wind, the particles that the sun blasts out into the solar system. Earth has a moderately strong magnetic field; without it life would not be possible because the solar wind would ravage the surface of the planet.

    Charon does not have a magnetic field, so there are no blue lines in the data returned in the control room.
    Mars has a weak magnetic field.
    Jupiter has an extremely strong magnetic field, as shown by the large number of lines and their distance from the planet.

    Seismograph


    Seismographs work only on worlds with hard surfaces. They measure movement of the ground, and are used especially to study earthquakes.


    Deimos has almost no seismic activity.
    Mercury has a moderate amount of seismic activity.
    Io has a great deal of seismic activity because of the pull of Jupiter.

    Thermometers

    Two types of data are returned from the thermometers: the average temperature in Kelvin, and the fluctuation in temperature over a given time period. Thermometers can only be used on landers because they must come into contact with the atmosphere they are measuring.



    The average temperature on Mars is slightly below freezing (freezing = 273K). Over a 24 hour period, Mars warms and cools, suggesting that their days are warmer than their nights, just like on Earth.
    This data is collected as a probe plunges through Saturn's atmosphere. The temperature rises on the way down, so there is no average temperature. Presumably after 8 hours the probe would be destroyed by the incredible pressure of Saturn's atmosphere.

    Spectrographs

    Spectrographs collect samples of substances in the atmosphere or on the surface of a world, then conduct tests to determine their composition. The data returned show the substances (11 elements and 4 compounds) and give an idea of the percentage of each. Students must use the spectral database to figure out what the elements are. The four compounds listed are, from left to right, water, sulfur dioxide, carbon dioxide, and methane.


    Io is rich in sulfur and sulfur dioxide.
    Like most gas giants, Neptune is rich in hydrogen and helium.

    RADAR

    RADAR is used to map the surface of a world from space. In other words, it can be used to measure the height of mountains and depth of valleys or seas. This is not a very useful instrument for data collection in Alien Rescue because students do not need this information to solve the problem.


    Callisto appears to have a great deal of area "at sea level," with many large areas suggesting deep craters.
    Like the other gas giants, Uranus does not have a surface that RADAR can bounce images off of.

    Infrared Camera

    Infrared cameras are used to measure heat. They work on flyby and orbiter probes, so they provide a good alternative to thermometers if a lander is not being used. Also, while thermometers gather data about one spot on a world, infrared cameras allow an opportunity to compare regions.


    Europa's surface temperature to be well-below freezing. It appears it is slightly warmer at the equator than at the polls.
    The side of Mercury that faces the sun is extremely hot, while the side facing away from the sun is much cooler.
